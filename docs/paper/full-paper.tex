\begin{abstract}
Smooth humanoid locomotion is often pursued by adding action-rate or
torque-rate penalties to the reward, but these penalties regularize realized
behavior only indirectly and require task-specific scalar tuning. We study
policy local sensitivity: how strongly the policy map amplifies observation
perturbations into action changes. Under a shared Humanoid-Gym rough-terrain
protocol, we compare three interventions: SC-PPO, which enforces a hard
policy-Jacobian constraint with a PID-Lagrangian multiplier; a locally adapted
LCP-style soft Jacobian/Lipschitz penalty; and a revised action-rate reward
heuristic. In a five-seed Isaac Gym evaluation, the soft penalty achieves zero
fall rate, lower velocity error, lower action jitter, higher return, and lower
measured policy sensitivity than SC-PPO, while the heuristic has slightly lower
joint acceleration. In matched five-seed MuJoCo replay, the soft penalty has the
lowest action jitter, whereas the heuristic obtains lower mean joint
acceleration and higher return. Observation-noise, actuator-bandwidth, and
\texttt{hfield\_moderate} diagnostics support the same interpretation:
policy-local-sensitivity regularization is best understood as a policy-output
smoothness mechanism, not as a sufficient condition for improving every
closed-loop metric.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Reinforcement-learning locomotion policies can satisfy task reward while
producing dynamically rough behavior. In humanoid locomotion this appears as
high-frequency action variation, large joint accelerations, and policies whose
smoothness depends on simulator details. A policy may perform well in its
training engine yet respond sharply to small observation or contact-solver
differences when replayed in another physics engine.

The standard engineering response is reward shaping. Action-rate, torque-rate,
joint-acceleration, and similar penalties are simple to implement and often
effective. However, the smoothness trade-off then becomes a scalar tuning
problem. The policy is not directly constrained to be locally stable as a map
from observations to actions; it is penalized only after it has emitted actions.

This paper evaluates whether policy local sensitivity provides an effective and
interpretable target for smooth humanoid control. The central object is the local
derivative of policy output with respect to policy observations. If a policy
strongly amplifies small observation differences into action differences, then
simulator-specific contact and integration differences may appear as action
jitter and dynamic roughness. Regularizing this amplification should therefore
improve policy-output smoothness, although its effect on downstream closed-loop
metrics depends on the enforcement mechanism and the control stack.

We study this question through a mechanism comparison. The evaluated methods
include a hard SC-PPO policy-Jacobian constraint, a soft LCP-style
Jacobian/Lipschitz penalty, and a revised action-rate reward heuristic. The
LCP-style penalty performs best among the sensitivity-based methods in the
present same-task evaluation, while the heuristic is stronger on downstream
MuJoCo joint acceleration and return. SC-PPO provides a complementary
hard-constraint condition that isolates the behavior of PID-Lagrangian
enforcement under seed variation, checkpoint selection, and cross-engine replay.

The contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item We formulate policy local sensitivity as a smooth-control
  mechanism for humanoid locomotion.
  \item We compare hard policy-sensitivity enforcement, soft
  LCP-style Jacobian/Lipschitz regularization, and a revised action-rate
  heuristic under the same five-seed Isaac Gym protocol.
  \item We perform matched five-seed MuJoCo replay and show a metric split:
  LCP-style regularization has the lowest action jitter, while the heuristic
  has lower mean joint acceleration and higher mean return.
  \item We report robustness analyses covering paired bootstrap uncertainty,
  selected-vs-final checkpoint dependence, local LCP coefficient sensitivity,
  controlled policy perturbations, observation-noise and actuator-bandwidth
  robustness without retraining, and a diagnostic \texttt{hfield\_moderate}
  second-setting replay.
  \item We provide feasibility checks for OmniSafe PPO-Lag and local
  CPO-style updates as context for alternative constrained-RL implementations.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

Constrained RL separates reward maximization from costs or limits. CPO
formulates policy optimization under explicit constraints~\citep{Achiam2017CPO},
while Safety Gym and PPO-Lagrangian practice provide common infrastructure for
cost-aware policy learning~\citep{Ray2019SafetyGym}. PID-Lagrangian methods
address multiplier overshoot and responsiveness in constrained deep
RL~\citep{Stooke2020PID}. OmniSafe packages many safe-RL algorithms behind a
common infrastructure~\citep{Ji2024OmniSafe}. Recent humanoid work also uses
constrained optimization to regulate physical costs such as
energy~\citep{Huang2026ECO}. This paper belongs to that cost/reward separation
lineage, but the cost placement is different: the SC-PPO cost is actor-internal
and depends on the derivative of the current policy with respect to observations
during PPO updates.

Smoothness in learned locomotion is commonly encouraged through reward terms on
action rate, torque rate, or acceleration. These methods are practical and form
strong reward-shaping baselines. A different family regularizes the policy map
itself. Spectral normalization constrains network Lipschitz
constants~\citep{Miyato2018SN}, and LayerNorm changes hidden activation
scaling~\citep{Ba2016LayerNorm}. LCP directly targets smooth humanoid
locomotion through a Lipschitz/Jacobian-style policy
regularizer~\citep{Chen2024LCP}, with adjacent work exploring spectral-normalization
variants for Lipschitz-constrained policies~\citep{Shin2025SNLCP}. We evaluate
a same-task LCP-style adaptation under Humanoid-Gym rough terrain; it is not an
official reproduction of the original LCP checkpoints, codebase, or evaluation
protocol.

Cross-engine replay provides an intermediate validation setting between
single-engine evaluation and hardware testing. Isaac Gym enables high-throughput
GPU simulation for robot learning~\citep{Makoviychuk2021IsaacGym}, while MuJoCo
uses a different physics engine and contact implementation~\citep{Todorov2012MuJoCo}.
Humanoid-Gym supplies the training scaffold and zero-shot transfer
context~\citep{Gu2024HumanoidGym}. We train and evaluate in Isaac Gym, then
replay selected checkpoints in aligned MuJoCo to test whether smoothness metrics
are robust to simulator changes.
This sim-to-sim role is related to work on robustness to dynamics mismatch,
such as dynamics randomization~\citep{Peng2018DynamicsRandomization}, but our
use of MuJoCo is deliberately narrower: a no-retraining sim-to-sim diagnostic
for selected policy checkpoints.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Shared PPO Task}

All primary methods use the same Humanoid-Gym rough-terrain locomotion task and
PPO backbone~\citep{Schulman2017PPO}. The robot is an H1-class humanoid with
12 DoF. The main evaluation uses seeds 11, 17, 23, 29, and 31, a
512-environment training budget, and a checkpoint sweep over the trained policy.
Evaluation reports fall rate, velocity-tracking error, joint-acceleration
$L_2$, action-jitter $L_2$, episode return, and policy-sensitivity measurements
where available.

The selected-checkpoint protocol is fixed before comparison: checkpoints are
filtered by task validity and then selected by a smoothness-first rule.
Final-checkpoint behavior is reported separately in the checkpoint robustness
analysis.

\subsection{SC-PPO Hard Policy-Sensitivity Constraint}

SC-PPO introduces a constraint cost based on policy local sensitivity. For a
policy mean $\mu_\theta(o)$, the local cost is the Frobenius norm of the
observation-to-action Jacobian:
\begin{equation}
  C(o) = \left\| \frac{\partial \mu_\theta(o)}{\partial o} \right\|_F .
\end{equation}
The batch cost for multiplier updates is a tail-sensitive statistic:
\begin{equation}
  J_C(\theta) = Q_{0.90}\left(\{C(o_i)\}\right).
\end{equation}
SC-PPO optimizes a Lagrangian objective
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}(\theta,\lambda) =
  J_R(\theta) - \lambda \left(J_C(\theta) - d\right),
\end{equation}
with threshold $d=3.8$, $\lambda_{\mathrm{init}}=0.5$, quantile aggregation,
and \texttt{lower\_bound\_clamp} PID integral handling. Heuristic smoothness
rewards are disabled in the SC-PPO condition, so smoothness pressure comes from the
Jacobian constraint rather than reward shaping.

\subsection{LCP-Style Soft Jacobian/Lipschitz Penalty}

The LCP-style method uses a fixed soft penalty on the policy-gradient quantity
$\|\nabla_o \log \pi_\theta(a \mid o)\|^2$, following the mechanism family of
Lipschitz-constrained policies~\citep{Chen2024LCP}. The local implementation
computes this penalty on PPO minibatch state-action samples with
\texttt{create\_graph=True}, uses a random subsample of actor observations for
computational tractability, and applies \texttt{lcp\_weight = 0.002}. It does
not use a Lagrange multiplier and disables heuristic smoothness rewards. It
shares the same task, checkpoint sweep, metric schema, and MuJoCo replay bridge
as SC-PPO and the heuristic.

This condition is the closest literature-aligned policy-sensitivity baseline in
our experimental stack, but it is not an official LCP reproduction. The public
LCP/MimicKit stack uses a different task, robot, checkpoint setup, and
evaluation path; we therefore label this method LCP-style rather than claiming
official parity.
Throughout the paper, ``LCP-style'' denotes this locally adapted soft
Jacobian/Lipschitz penalty, not an official LCP reproduction; when context is
clear, we refer to the same row as the soft penalty.

\subsection{Revised Heuristic Reward-Shaping Anchor}

The heuristic method is PPO with a tuned action-rate penalty under the formal
protocol revision. It represents the practical reward-shaping approach that the
policy-sensitivity methods must compare against. Because it is strong in MuJoCo,
especially on joint acceleration and return, it is treated as a primary
baseline.

\subsection{OmniSafe PPO-Lag and CPO Feasibility Checks}

OmniSafe PPO-Lag was evaluated as a feasibility check. The adapter,
cost bridge, policy evaluation bridge, and update-hook smoke paths were
implemented, but the three-seed end-to-end experiment did not produce
task-valid locomotion. We therefore exclude OmniSafe PPO-Lag from the primary
baseline table. The result indicates that a standard environment-side PPO-Lag
interface does not directly support this actor-internal Jacobian cost without
algorithm-level hooks.

A local CPO-style path was also tested as an implementation-feasibility check.
Tensor and one-update checks passed, and a short training-loop diagnostic
produced finite accepted updates, but the evaluated checkpoints did not satisfy
the task-validity criteria. We therefore do not include CPO in the main baseline
table.

\section{Experimental Protocol}

The primary Isaac comparison uses five seeds, checkpoint sweeps, and the same
metric schema for LCP-style, SC-PPO, and heuristic methods. MuJoCo replay uses the
selected Isaac checkpoint for each seed under the aligned \texttt{isaac\_mainline}
protocol with 20 episodes of 20 seconds and \texttt{joint\_reset\_noise = 0.1}.

Metrics are fall rate, velocity-tracking error, joint-acceleration $L_2$,
action-jitter $L_2$, episode return, and policy sensitivity. Fall, velocity
error, joint acceleration, action jitter, and sensitivity are lower-is-better;
episode return is higher-is-better. Policy sensitivity and violation rate are
auxiliary measurements of policy-local behavior rather than task reward.
Episode return is not normalized across engines, so Isaac and MuJoCo return
values should be compared only within the same engine.

The primary comparison consists of the LCP-style soft penalty, SC-PPO 3.8
PID-Lagrangian, and the revised heuristic. Robustness analyses include paired
bootstrap uncertainty, selected-vs-final checkpoint dependence, and LCP
coefficient sensitivity, controlled policy perturbations, and no-retraining
observation noise. We also include no-retraining MuJoCo actuator-bandwidth
stress and \texttt{hfield\_moderate} replay as diagnostic checks.
Additional analyses examine OmniSafe PPO-Lag migration, local CPO-style checks,
and alternative mechanisms that did not meet the main task-validity criteria.

\subsection{Robustness Analyses}

The statistical robustness analysis is descriptive rather than a large-sample
significance test. With five seeds, it supports effect directions and
uncertainty intervals rather than binary accept/reject conclusions. The most
conservative robust comparison is that LCP has stable advantages over the
SC-PPO hard-constraint condition on Isaac fall rate, velocity error, return,
sensitivity, and MuJoCo action jitter.

The selected-vs-final checkpoint analysis shows that LCP is close to final-only
behavior. Only seed 11 selects checkpoint 300 instead of final checkpoint 400,
and selected-to-final aggregate deltas are small. SC-PPO is more
dynamic-selection-sensitive: final checkpoints improve velocity and return but
worsen joint acceleration and jitter. The heuristic is task-selection-sensitive:
final checkpoints improve velocity and return slightly but increase fall rate
and dynamic roughness. Table~\ref{tab:checkpoint-robustness} gives the compact
delta read. For lower-is-better metrics, positive values mean the final
checkpoint is worse than the selected checkpoint.

The LCP coefficient analysis tests weights 0.001, 0.002, and 0.004 on the same
five-seed set. The smallest weight appears under-regularized, with nonzero fall
rate, higher measured sensitivity, and rougher selected policies. The largest
weight lowers measured sensitivity further but worsens velocity error, joint
acceleration, and action jitter relative to 0.002, while return is nearly tied.
This does not prove global hyperparameter optimality, but it shows that the main
LCP condition is not an isolated single-point accident within the tested
neighborhood.

The SC-PPO threshold is a local design choice rather than a globally optimized
constant. A threshold sweep over nearby shared values found seed-dependent
behavior: lower settings over-constrained some seeds, while looser settings
improved one seed but degraded others. No tested shared threshold in that
local grid passed all task-validity and smoothness gates. We therefore use
threshold 3.8 as the documented hard-constraint setting. A matched plain-dual
comparison at the same threshold also failed the task-validity criteria,
supporting PID-Lagrangian as the stronger within-family SC-PPO condition without
establishing the independent necessity of every PID term.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{What the Mechanism Comparison Shows}

The experiments support policy local sensitivity as an informative
smooth-control target, while also showing that the enforcement mechanism
substantially affects performance. The clearest reading comes from separating
three levels: the policy map, the policy-output stream, and downstream
closed-loop dynamics. Under the same task and metric schema, the soft penalty is
stronger than the evaluated hard-constraint SC-PPO implementation on most Isaac
metrics and on MuJoCo action jitter.

Soft regularization may be easier to optimize in PPO because the penalty is
present throughout actor updates with a fixed coefficient. SC-PPO exposes the
behavior of hard enforcement under the same policy-sensitivity objective: the
constraint is interpretable, but PID-Lagrangian multiplier dynamics and
checkpoint behavior are seed-sensitive. In this comparison, SC-PPO is therefore
best read as a diagnostic hard-constraint condition that explains why soft
regularization is easier to use here, rather than as the strongest competing
method.

For practitioners, the results suggest that adding a fixed soft Jacobian penalty
can be a simple alternative to reward-only smoothness tuning in a PPO locomotion
stack. It still requires choosing a coefficient, but it avoids balancing several
smoothness reward weights directly against the task objective.

\subsection{Why the Heuristic Still Matters}

The revised heuristic is a strong reward-shaping baseline. In matched MuJoCo
replay it outperforms LCP on mean joint acceleration and mean return, even
though LCP achieves lower action jitter. This trade-off is important because it
separates policy-output smoothness from downstream closed-loop dynamics.

Action jitter is the metric closest to the policy-output stream, while joint
acceleration and return depend on the full closed loop. A reward-shaped policy
can therefore produce better downstream dynamics even when its policy map is
more sensitive. Conversely, a low-sensitivity policy can produce cleaner action
commands without improving every downstream metric.

\subsection{Sim-to-Sim Scope}

MuJoCo replay changes physics-engine details without changing the trained
policy checkpoint. The sim-to-sim result therefore tests whether smoothness
metrics are robust to simulator changes, but it does not establish sim-to-real
robustness. Domain randomization, system identification, actuator modeling, and
hardware tests remain outside the present scope.

\section{Limitations}

The LCP-style method is a local same-task adaptation, not an official
reproduction of the original LCP code, checkpoints, or evaluation protocol. The
results therefore compare mechanisms under this Humanoid-Gym protocol rather
than establishing parity with or superiority over the official LCP
implementation.

The primary evidence uses one robot morphology, one rough-terrain task, five
training seeds, and selected-checkpoint evaluation. The selected-vs-final
analysis reduces this concern for LCP but does not eliminate checkpoint
dependence for all methods. The \texttt{hfield\_moderate} replay is a diagnostic
second-setting check, not a broad multi-terrain benchmark. A random-stairs
stress attempt was not task-valid enough to serve as claim-grade multi-terrain
evidence.

The smoothness-mechanism hyperparameters are local choices, not globally
optimized constants. The LCP coefficient is supported by a narrow
0.001/0.002/0.004 five-seed neighborhood test, and the SC-PPO threshold is
supported only as the evaluated hard-constraint setting after a local threshold
sweep. Broad hyperparameter optimality remains outside this study.

MuJoCo replay is sim-to-sim evidence only. There is no real-robot validation,
calibrated actuator model, system identification, or multi-terrain/multi-robot
study. The actuator low-pass proxy stress test is an additional simulation
stress test; its high-fall $\tau=0.10$ setting should not be interpreted as a
hardware-transfer ranking.

The mechanism explanation remains local and partly correlational. The policy
perturbation analysis gives controlled local policy-output evidence, and the
matched MuJoCo decomposition supports a control-path metric split, but neither
proves a closed-loop causal intervention from sensitivity to joint acceleration
or return.

The statistical analysis uses five seeds and should be interpreted as
descriptive uncertainty estimation rather than a large-sample hypothesis test.

External constrained-RL coverage remains incomplete. OmniSafe PPO-Lag was
tested as a limited-scope interface feasibility check and did not produce task-valid
locomotion, but this does not imply that OmniSafe, PPO-Lag, CPO, or external
constrained RL algorithms broadly fail. A local CPO-style path was also tested
through autograd/HVP, one-update, and short training-loop checks. The tensor
and one-update checks passed, and the training loop produced finite accepted
updates, but the evaluated checkpoints did not satisfy the task-validity
criteria. CPO-style training therefore remains future work rather than a primary
baseline in this study.

\section{Conclusion}

Policy-local-sensitivity regularization provides an effective mechanism for
improving policy-output smoothness in humanoid locomotion, but its downstream
effects depend on how the regularization is enforced. In the present same-task
evaluation, a fixed soft LCP-style Jacobian/Lipschitz penalty is stronger than
the evaluated hard SC-PPO constraint on most Isaac metrics and on MuJoCo action
jitter. The revised action-rate heuristic retains advantages on MuJoCo joint
acceleration and return, showing that reward shaping can still produce favorable
closed-loop dynamics. These results motivate mechanism-level
comparisons of policy sensitivity, soft regularization, and reward shaping,
while leaving official LCP parity, broader hyperparameter optimization, and
hardware validation for future work.

\section{SC-PPO Additional Comparisons}

The SC-PPO hard-constraint condition uses threshold 3.8, quantile aggregation, and
PID-Lagrangian multiplier updates. These settings define the evaluated
hard-constraint condition rather than an exhaustively optimized SC-PPO
configuration. The local threshold sweep found no shared nearby threshold that
passed all task-validity and smoothness gates, and the matched plain-dual
comparison at the same threshold also failed the task-validity criteria. These
results support the chosen PID-Lagrangian SC-PPO condition for mechanism
comparison, but they do not establish global threshold optimality or the
independent necessity of every PID term.
